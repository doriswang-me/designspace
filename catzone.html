01 / Overview

The Project

For my thesis project at the California College of the Arts (Spring 2024), I designed an app that helps people know more about the street cats in their community — and collectively take care of them. The project includes a poster, an animated intro video, a brochure, and a fully prototyped mobile app accessible via QR code.

02 / Problem Statement

Why Street Cats?

I'm a cat person. I noticed that there are so many street cats in my neighborhood — and many neighbors feel the same connection. But we don't know these cats well. We don't know their names, health status, or how to coordinate care.

In many U.S. neighborhoods, street cats are a common presence — often roaming without adequate food, shelter, or healthcare. Unlike pets, most street cats cannot be placed for adoption — they need a different kind of community-scale solution.

How Might We
"How might we help residents understand, connect with, and collectively support their neighborhood street cats?"
Pain Point 1
No shared cat knowledge
Residents see the same cats daily but have no way to learn their names, health history, or current needs. Information is isolated, never shared.
Pain Point 2
Uncoordinated care
Multiple neighbors may want to feed or help the same cat, but without coordination — leading to overfeeding some cats and neglecting others.
Pain Point 3
Fear of engagement
People want to interact with street cats but fear disease, aggression, or vaccination status. Missing information creates hesitation where care could thrive.
03 / User Research

Who We're Designing For

I conducted structured interviews with 5 participants representing a range of engagement levels with street cats — from casual observers to regular feeders. The goal was to surface their motivations, concerns, and unmet needs around neighborhood cat interaction.

User interviews with 5 participants — surfacing motivations from casual curiosity to active care routines
Research Insight 1
People desire engagement with street cats
All 5 participants expressed a desire to interact — but felt held back by not knowing the cats' names, personalities, or health status. Connection was there; information was missing.
Research Insight 2
People want more details about street cats
Participants wanted to know breed, vaccinations, feeding history, and behavioral traits before engaging. A shared profile system would directly address this need.
Research Insight 3
Community coordination is missing
Regular feeders like Participant E wanted to create shared routines with neighbors. No existing platform served this hyperlocal, animal-centered community need.
04 / Ideation & Concept

Why an App?

I explored multiple formats — posters, community boards, websites. A mobile app emerged as the ideal solution because it uniquely satisfies the user's core needs: convenience, real-time notifications, and dynamic information across many scenarios.

Ideation framework mapping app benefits and services against user wants, needs, and fears
RATIONALE 01
Convenience
Easy to carry — usable in-the-moment whether spotting a cat on a walk or logging a feeding from home.
RATIONALE 02
Smart Notifications
Analyzes information and pushes contextual alerts — feeding reminders, unseen cat alerts, community updates.
RATIONALE 03
Dynamic Information
Displays diverse, live information — GPS maps, photo logs, feeding schedules, community posts — all in one place.
05 / User Story & Journey Map

Following Jessica

I built a primary persona — Jessica, a programmer who just moved to Skyline Community. She's a cat lover but feels uncertain about approaching unfamiliar street cats. Her journey from discovery to delight maps the full emotional arc the app is designed to support.

Storyboard — Jessica moves to a new community, discovers street cats, and uses Catzone to confidently connect with them
User journey map — tracking Jessica's emotions across 4 phases: Before → Register → Use → After. Key friction: download wait time. Key delight: finding detailed cat info.
06 / Information Architecture & Wireframes

Structuring the Experience

Based on research insights, I mapped the app's information architecture into four primary flows: Browse Cat Profiles, Add Details, Catzone Community, and Specific Cat Profile. Wireframes were drawn for each screen before any visual design began.

Information architecture — four primary flows from Homepage: Browse Cats Profiles → Specific Cat Profile, Add Details, and Catzone Community
Full wireframe set covering all 4 app sections: Onboarding, Locate Where You Are, Meet With Cats, and Upload Thoughts
07 / Design System

Colors, Typography & Tools

The Catzone design system uses a soft, approachable palette — blues and teals for trust and community, warm amber and rose for warmth and care. Typography is set in Poppins for its rounded, friendly character. Designed in Figma with Photoshop for image treatment.

Design system — 5-color palette, Poppins typeface at 4 sizes, and tool stack (Figma + Photoshop)

Key Features

The final high-fidelity design covers 4 core experiences — onboarding with interactive animation, cat profile browsing, community engagement, and precise GPS location. Each screen is crafted to reduce friction and make caring for street cats feel effortless and rewarding.

09 / Reflection & Takeaway

What I Learned

💡
Personal Empathy Drives Authentic Research
Starting from genuine lived experience as a cat person gave the research honest direction. When the designer cares deeply about the problem, every interview question becomes more precise and every insight lands with more weight.
🏘️
Community Design Requires Thinking at Network Scale
Unlike single-user products, Catzone needed to serve a web of neighbors with different roles. This meant designing for trust between strangers, shared data ownership, and contribution models that feel rewarding rather than obligatory.
🎨
Multi-Deliverable Projects Sharpen Core Messaging
Producing a poster, video, brochure, and prototype for the same concept forced me to distill the core idea repeatedly. That constraint made every design decision — from icon choice to color palette — more intentional and defensible.
📊
IA Before UI is Non-Negotiable
Mapping the information architecture and wireframing every screen before touching Figma ensured the product logic was solid. Visual design became much faster and more purposeful once the structure was locked.
